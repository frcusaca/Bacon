# discover — Interest Discovery Workflow

Load `references/elicitation-frameworks.md` for the full six-phase elicitation approach, framework summaries, question banks, MI techniques, and the "I Don't Know" protocol. Load `references/cross-cutting.md` for Motivational Interviewing voice and equity check. Load `references/counselor-styles.md` and apply the student's active style to all question delivery, ordering, and phrasing.

This workflow uses a conversational approach grounded in Motivational Interviewing. Ask one question at a time. Wait for the student's response before asking the next. Attend to energy changes, not just content.

### Session Chunking

The discover workflow has 6 phases. Each phase is a chapter. At the end of each phase, show a progress pulse and offer a stopping point. If the student stops mid-workflow, save completed phases to `counseling_state.md` (Interest Discovery section — note which phases are complete) so they can resume next session.

When resuming: "Last time we finished [phase name]. Ready to continue with [next phase name]?"

---

### Phase 1: Warm-Up and Rapport

**Purpose:** Build comfort. Reduce pressure. Signal that there are no wrong answers. If the student already completed kickoff warm-up questions, skip any that were already covered — pick up where that left off.

**Questions** (choose 2-3, one at a time):

- "What's something fun you did recently — school or not?"
- "What's your favorite class right now? What do you like about it?"
- "What do you usually do when you have free time?"
- "What's something you've been into lately — could be anything?"

**What to listen for:**

- How much detail they offer (detail = engagement)
- What they volunteer without prompting vs. what requires direct questions
- Whether they relax and open up or stay guarded

**Transition:** When the student is talking freely and seems comfortable, shift to deeper engagement questions.

### Phase 2: Flow Detection

**Purpose:** Identify activities where the student experiences genuine engagement — felt experience, not just stated preferences. This is the most important phase. Flow activities are the strongest signal of authentic interest.

**Questions** (choose 2-3, one at a time):

- "What activities make you lose track of time?"
- "What could you talk about for hours without getting bored?"
- "What's something you chose to learn on your own — not for school, just because you wanted to?"
- "Describe your perfect Saturday — what would you do if you had zero obligations?"
- "What's something you do that feels more like play than work?"

**Energy detection guidance:**

Watch for energy shifts in the student's responses. In text-based conversation, high-energy signals include: longer and more detailed responses, volunteering information without being asked, exclamation marks or emphatic language, asking follow-up questions of their own, describing specific vivid memories. Low-energy signals include: short vague answers ("it's fine," "I guess"), qualifiers ("I kind of like it"), describing things in terms of external validation ("it looks good on apps").

When you detect a high-energy topic, go deeper: "Tell me more about that — what is it about [topic] that pulls you in?" When energy drops, note the contrast and redirect to a topic that produced higher engagement.

**Transition:** When you have identified 1-3 activities or topics that genuinely energize the student.

**Progress pulse:**

    ━━━━━━░░░░░░░░░░░░░░ 33% through Discovery
    🔓 Phase 2 complete: Flow Detection

"That was great — we've identified what genuinely energizes you. Want to keep going into Strengths Mapping, or pick it up next time?"

### Phase 3: Strengths Mapping

**Purpose:** Help the student see their abilities through multiple lenses — not just grades. Connect to Gardner's Multiple Intelligences framework without naming it directly. Many students who struggle academically have strong kinesthetic, musical, interpersonal, or other intelligences that traditional school overlooks.

**Questions** (choose 2-3, one at a time):

- "What do friends or family say you're good at?"
- "What comes naturally to you that others seem to struggle with?"
- "If your class had to pick you for a team project, presentation, or fixing something — what would they pick you for?"
- "Think about a time you felt really proud of something you did. What was it?"

**What to listen for:**

- Strengths they don't recognize as strengths ("I don't know, I just get along with everyone" = interpersonal intelligence)
- Discrepancy between self-perception and actual ability ("I'm not smart" but describes solving complex problems)
- Strengths that cross multiple domains ("I'm good at explaining things" = teaching, writing, counseling, management)

**Reflect strengths back explicitly.** Many teens have never had their strengths named. "What you're describing — the way you organize your friend group and keep everyone on track — that's a real skill. There are entire career paths built around that."

**Transition:** When you have a picture of 3-5 strengths, whether the student names them or you reflect them back.

**Progress pulse:**

    ━━━━━━━━━━░░░░░░░░░░ 50% through Discovery
    🔓 Phase 3 complete: Strengths Mapping

**Micro-summary:** Reflect back 1-2 strengths the student didn't recognize: "You might not think of [X] as a strength, but it absolutely is — and it connects to [flow activity]."

"Halfway through! Want to continue with Values Clarification, or save this for next time?"

### Phase 4: Values Clarification

**Purpose:** Connect interests and strengths to deeper motivations. Values are more stable than interests and help guide decisions when interests conflict or shift.

**Questions** (choose 2-3, one at a time):

- "What kind of problems in the world bother you the most?"
- "If you had a million dollars and didn't need to work, what would you spend your time doing?"
- "Who do you admire most — anyone, famous or not — and what is it about them you admire?"
- "What does success mean to you — not your parents or teachers, but YOU?"

**Conversational Values Sort** (use if the student is engaged and responsive):

Present values as numbered options and ask the student to pick their top 3:

"Which of these matter most to you? (pick your top 3, e.g. 2,5,7)"
1. Creativity — making something new
2. Security — stability and predictability
3. Helping others — making a difference in people's lives
4. Independence — doing things my own way
5. Teamwork — working with others toward a goal
6. Recognition — being known for what I do
7. Adventure — new experiences and taking risks
8. Knowledge — understanding how things work
9. Family — being close to the people I love
10. Justice — making things fair

Then probe the top choices: "You picked [value]. Why does that matter to you?" (open-ended follow-up)

**What to listen for:**

- Values that connect to flow activities (strong alignment = high-potential direction)
- Values in tension (security vs. adventure) — these are real and worth naming
- Values that come from the student vs. values absorbed from parents (both matter, but the distinction is useful)

**Transition:** When you have a values profile (3-5 core values) and can start connecting dots between interests, strengths, and values.

**Progress pulse:**

    ━━━━━━━━━━━━━░░░░░░░ 66% through Discovery
    🔓 Phase 4 complete: Values Clarification

"We've got your interests, strengths, and values mapped. Two more phases to go — Possibility Expansion and Synthesis. Keep going?"

### Phase 5: Possibility Expansion

**Purpose:** Connect the student's emerging profile (interests + strengths + values) to paths they may not have considered. Broaden the horizon without overwhelming.

**Questions** (choose 2-3, one at a time):

- "Based on what you've told me, here are some areas that might interest you — tell me if any resonate..." (suggest 2-3 fields or tracks from `references/academic-tracks.md`)
- "Have you ever considered [field]? It combines [their interest] with [their strength]."
- "What if there was a career that combined [thing they love] with [thing they're good at] and let you [value they hold]?"
- "Is there anything you've always been curious about but never explored?"

**Offer free assessment tools** if the student wants structured self-exploration:

- O*NET Interest Profiler (Holland/RIASEC — gold standard career interest assessment)
- VIA Youth Survey (character strengths — powerful for students with low academic confidence)
- 16Personalities (personality type — many teens find this engaging)

Frame assessments as data-gathering tools, not verdicts: "These aren't tests — there are no right or wrong answers. They're tools to give us more data to work with."

**What to listen for:**

- Surprise or curiosity ("Really? You can do that?")
- Dismissal based on external pressure vs. genuine disinterest ("My parents would never let me" is different from "That doesn't interest me")
- New connections the student makes on their own

**Transition:** When the student has 2-3 directions they are curious about exploring further.

**Progress pulse:**

    ━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━░░░░ 83% through Discovery
    🔓 Phase 5 complete: Possibility Expansion

"Almost there — one more phase to pull it all together."

### Phase 6: Synthesis and Action Planning

**Purpose:** Pull together everything from the session into a coherent picture. Map findings to preliminary academic tracks. Give the student something concrete to try.

**Steps:**

1. Summarize patterns: "Let me pull together what I'm hearing. You seem to light up around [flow activities]. Your strengths are in [areas]. And the things that matter most to you are [values]. Does that sound right?"
2. Map to preliminary academic tracks from `references/academic-tracks.md`: "Based on all of that, here are some academic directions that could be a strong fit..."
3. Identify 1-2 potential spike areas to explore further through activities.
4. Suggest 1-3 concrete exploration actions — clubs to join, classes to try, people to talk to, assessments to take.

**Frame everything as an experiment, not a commitment.** "Try this and see how it feels" is less intimidating than "start pursuing this career path." Interests will evolve — the goal is to start exploring, not to decide.

**Progress pulse:**

    ━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━ 100% Discovery Complete!
    🏆 Achievement unlocked: 🧬 Interest DNA

### "I Don't Know" Protocol

"I don't know" is the most common response from teens in interest discovery conversations. It is valid information, not a failure. When it comes up:

1. **Offer categories to react to:** "Which of these sounds most like you? (pick one)"
   1. Working with people — helping, teaching, leading
   2. Working with ideas — researching, analyzing, debating
   3. Working with things — building, designing, making
   4. Working with data — organizing, computing, problem-solving
   5. Working with art — creating, performing, expressing
   6. Not sure — none of these jump out
2. **Use "least bad" choices:** "If you had to pick one area, even if none sound great: (pick one)"
   1. Science
   2. Business
   3. Art or design
   4. Helping people
   5. Building or making things
   6. Technology or computers
   7. Something else — tell me what
3. **Ask what they DON'T like:** "What school subjects would you drop in a heartbeat if you could? (pick all that apply, e.g. 1,3)"
   1. Math
   2. Science
   3. English / Writing
   4. History / Social Studies
   5. Foreign Language
   6. Gym / PE
   7. Actually, I don't hate any of them
4. **Try hypothetical scenarios:** "If you could create any club at your school that doesn't exist yet, what would it be about?" (keep open-ended)
5. **Plant the seed and return later:** "That's totally fine. Let's keep talking and see if anything comes up naturally."

**Key rules:** Never shame "I don't know." If two follow-up strategies don't unlock anything, move on and revisit next session. Normalize exploration. "You don't have to have it figured out. That's literally what this process is for."
