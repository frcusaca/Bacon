# financial — Financial Aid and Scholarship Workflow

Load `references/counselor-styles.md` and apply the student's active style to all question delivery, ordering, and phrasing.

### Step 1: Financial Context

Gather context with sensitivity — families have different comfort levels discussing finances.

1. "Has your family started talking about how to pay for college? (pick one)"
   1. Yes — we have a plan
   2. Some conversations but nothing concrete
   3. Not really — haven't gotten into it yet
   4. I'd rather not discuss family finances right now
   (If they pick 4, respect it: "Totally fine — I can walk you through how financial aid works in general so you know what's available when you're ready.")

2. "How important is financial aid in your college decision? (pick one)"
   1. Critical — need significant aid or scholarships to attend
   2. Important — want to minimize cost but have some flexibility
   3. A factor but not the top priority
   4. Not a major concern
   (Respect privacy — don't push beyond what they offer.)

3. "Is your family familiar with the financial aid process? (pick one)"
   1. Yes — a sibling or parent has been through it
   2. Somewhat — know the basics but not the details
   3. Not at all — this is all new territory
   (First-gen awareness — if they pick 3, surface the hidden curriculum proactively.)

If the student is uncomfortable discussing finances, acknowledge it: "Totally fine — we don't need to get into specifics. I can walk you through how financial aid works in general so you know what's available when you're ready."

Adapt depth based on grade level:
- **Freshman/Sophomore**: Light education — awareness of what exists, no urgency
- **Junior (spring)**: Action-oriented — run net price calculators, understand FAFSA timeline
- **Senior (fall)**: Execution mode — file FAFSA/CSS Profile, build scholarship calendar, compare offers

### Step 2: FAFSA and CSS Profile Education

Walk through both forms at appropriate depth for the student's grade:

**FAFSA (Free Application for Federal Student Aid)**:
- Universal — virtually all schools require it for any financial aid
- Opens October 1 each year. File as early as possible — some aid is first-come
- Free to file. Calculates the Student Aid Index (SAI) — what the government estimates the family can contribute
- Determines eligibility for: Pell Grants (free money), federal loans, work-study
- Uses prior-prior year tax data (e.g., 2024 taxes for 2026-27 aid year)

**CSS Profile (College Board)**:
- Required by ~200 mostly private schools for institutional (school's own) aid
- Opens October 1. Deadlines vary by school — check each target school
- More detailed than FAFSA: includes home equity, business assets, non-custodial parent income
- Cost: $25 first school, $16 each additional (fee waivers available for low-income families)
- Unlocks access to $14+ billion in non-federal aid
- Check the student's college list to determine which schools require it

**EFC/SAI explanation**: "The SAI is an estimate of what the government thinks your family can afford. It's not perfect — many families find the number doesn't match their reality. That's where school-specific aid, scholarships, and appeals come in."

### Step 3: Net Price Calculators

For each school on the student's college list (or top 5-8 if the list is long):

1. Explain what net price means: "Sticker price minus grants and scholarships equals what you actually pay. The sticker price scares everyone — the net price is what matters."
2. Guide the student to each school's net price calculator (every school is required to have one on its website)
3. Explain what information they'll need: family income, assets, household size, number in college
4. If the student doesn't have financial details handy, suggest they ask a parent to run the calculators together
5. Record estimates in the state for comparison

Frame this as empowering, not stressful: "Running these calculators is one of the most useful things you can do — it turns a scary question mark into real numbers you can plan around."

### Step 4: Need-Blind and Full-Need Schools

Educate on the landscape of generous financial aid:

**Need-Blind Admissions**: School doesn't consider ability to pay when making admissions decisions. Examples: Harvard, MIT, Princeton, Yale, Amherst, Brown.

**Meets Full Demonstrated Need**: School covers 100% of the gap between what the family can pay and the cost of attendance. Only ~25 schools nationally do this.

**No-Loan Policies**: School meets need entirely with grants — no loans required. Harvard, Princeton, MIT, Brown, Stanford, and others.

**The key insight**: Only ~12 schools are need-blind AND meet full demonstrated need. These are the most financially generous schools in the country — and they're also among the most selective. For families where cost is a major factor, these schools deserve consideration even as reaches.

Cross-reference with the student's college list: "Let's check which of your schools have strong financial aid. [School X] meets full need. [School Y] is need-aware, which means financial need might be a factor in borderline admissions decisions."

### Step 5: Scholarship Strategy

Build a scholarship plan across four categories:

**Merit Scholarships** (from colleges):
- Many state universities offer automatic merit awards at GPA/test score thresholds
- Strong SAT/ACT scores are the most realistic way to increase merit aid
- Some schools offer "Presidential" or named scholarships requiring separate applications
- Check each target school's merit scholarship page

**Local Scholarships** (community-based):
- Less competition than national awards — higher odds of winning
- Ask the school counselor for a list of local scholarships
- Check: community foundations, Rotary/Lions Club, local businesses, employers, religious organizations
- Smaller amounts ($500-5,000) but they stack

**National Scholarships** (competitive):
- QuestBridge (full rides for high-achieving, low-income students)
- National Merit (based on PSAT score — semifinalist cutoffs vary by state)
- Gates Scholarship, Coca-Cola Scholars, Dell Scholars, Elks Most Valuable Student
- Search engines: Fastweb, College Board Scholarship Search, Bold.org, Scholarships.com

**Essay-Based Scholarships**:
- Many scholarships require essays — good practice for application writing
- Reuse and adapt Common App essay themes where prompts overlap
- Apply broadly — many have fewer applicants than expected
- Juniors and seniors should build a scholarship calendar with all deadlines

Help the student build a concrete calendar: "Let's map out which scholarships you're eligible for, when they're due, and which ones are the best use of your time."

### Step 6: Financial Aid Comparison and Negotiation

When aid letters arrive (typically December-April for seniors):

**How to read an aid package**:
- Separate grants/scholarships (free money) from loans (must repay) and work-study (must earn)
- Calculate the true net cost: total cost - grants/scholarships = what the family pays (out of pocket + loans)
- Check whether aid is renewable or one-time
- Look for hidden costs: travel, books, personal expenses, health insurance

**How to compare across schools**:
- Build a side-by-side comparison table with true net cost per school
- A school with a lower sticker price isn't always cheaper — a generous aid package at an expensive school can beat a cheaper school with less aid

**Financial aid negotiation** (professional judgment requests):
- Financial aid packages are not always final. Families can and should appeal when:
  - Financial circumstances have changed (job loss, medical expenses, divorce, retirement)
  - Another comparable school offered significantly more aid (provide the competing offer)
  - The SAI doesn't reflect the family's actual ability to pay
- Write a professional, factual appeal letter to the financial aid office
- This works more often than families expect — it is a normal part of the process
- Frame it to the student: "Negotiating financial aid isn't rude or greedy — it's expected. Financial aid officers deal with appeals all the time."
