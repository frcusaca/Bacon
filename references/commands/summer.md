# summer — Summer Program Strategy Workflow

Load `references/counselor-styles.md` and apply the student's active style to all question delivery, ordering, and phrasing.

### Step 1: Context

Gather the information needed to make grade-appropriate, spike-aligned recommendations.

1. "What grade are you in now — and what grade will you be going into next fall?" (determines which tier of programs and what kind of experience is appropriate)
2. Pull from state: spike area(s), interest discovery results, academic track
3. "Has your family discussed a budget for summer programs? Some of the best programs are free, but others cost $5,000-8,000." (surface financial context without assuming)
4. "What have you done in previous summers?" (pull from state if available — look for progression)
5. "Is there anything you already know you want to do this summer?" (respect existing plans before recommending)

If spike/interests aren't yet identified, suggest running `discover` first: "Summer plans work best when they connect to what you're passionate about. Want to spend a few minutes figuring that out before we plan your summer?"

### Step 2: Program Tier Education

Explain the landscape so the student can make informed decisions:

**Tier 1: Highly Selective and Free**
The programs admissions officers recognize by name. All are free or sliding-scale with stipends.

| Program | Focus | Duration | Selectivity |
|---------|-------|----------|-------------|
| RSI (Research Science Institute) | STEM research at MIT | 6 weeks | ~2-3% acceptance |
| SSP (Summer Science Program) | Astrophysics, biochem, genomics | 39 days | ~7% |
| MITES Summer (MIT) | STEM + entrepreneurship | 6 weeks | ~8% |
| Simons Summer Research | Research at Stony Brook | 8 weeks | ~5% |
| Clark Scholars (Texas Tech) | Intensive research | 7 weeks | ~3% |
| Stanford SIMR | Medical research | 8 weeks | ~5% |
| COSMOS (UC system) | STEM clusters | 4 weeks | ~15% |

These carry genuine admissions signal because they are merit-based and extremely competitive. Apply sophomore or junior winter (deadlines typically December-February).

**Tier 2: Highly Regarded (Paid or Selective)**

| Program | Focus | Cost | Notes |
|---------|-------|------|-------|
| LaunchX | Entrepreneurship | ~$5,000 | ~10% acceptance; build real startups |
| Yale Young Global Scholars | Multidisciplinary | ~$6,500 | STEM, humanities, social sciences |
| MIT Beaver Works (BWSI) | Applied engineering | Free | Autonomous vehicles, cybersecurity |
| Stanford SUMaC | Advanced math | ~$8,000 | Abstract algebra, number theory |
| Interlochen | Arts | ~$4,000-7,000 | Music, visual arts, theater, film |
| Bank of America Student Leaders | Leadership + nonprofit | Free | Community-focused |

Good signal, meaningful experience. Apply early — most have rolling or early deadlines.

**Tier 3: University Pre-College Programs (Paid)**
These provide campus experience and exploration but do not boost applications — admissions officers know they are pay-to-attend.

| Program | Cost | Notes |
|---------|------|-------|
| Harvard Pre-College | ~$6,100 | Good exposure; does NOT help Harvard admission |
| Stanford Pre-Collegiate Studies | ~$7,000-8,000 | Academic enrichment |
| Johns Hopkins Pre-College | Varies | Medicine, STEM, humanities |

Fine for personal growth and exploration. Not a substitute for Tier 1-2 or meaningful self-directed work.

**Self-Directed Alternatives**
Equally impressive when substantive — and available to every student regardless of budget:
- Start a meaningful project (blog, nonprofit, app, community initiative, research paper)
- Cold-email professors at local universities for research opportunities
- Get a real job (shows maturity, responsibility, and real-world skills)
- Deep independent study with a tangible output
- Volunteer with an organization you genuinely care about
- Create something: film, website, art series, community event, curriculum

"Admissions officers care about what you did and what it meant — not whether a program name is on the experience. A student who spent the summer building something real from scratch can be more compelling than one who attended a prestigious program."

### Step 3: Grade-Appropriate Recommendations

Tailor recommendations to where the student is in their journey:

**Rising Sophomore (finishing 9th grade)**:
- Explore broadly — this is not the summer to specialize
- First meaningful experience: part-time job, volunteering, camp, personal project
- Try something connected to emerging interests but without pressure to commit
- Research Tier 1/2 programs for the following summer (applications due in winter)
- "This summer is about trying things. Next summer is about going deep."

**Rising Junior (finishing 10th grade)**:
- Apply to Tier 1/2 selective programs (applications due December-February of sophomore year)
- Plan a self-directed project as backup if not accepted to formal programs
- Deepen the spike: if the student loves science, a lab experience; if they love writing, a publication
- This is the last "development" summer before senior year applications
- "Whatever you do this summer should connect to your story. It doesn't have to be a big-name program — it has to be meaningful."

**Rising Senior (finishing 11th grade)**:
- Most impactful summer for college applications — this is what the application will reference
- Meaningful experience that connects directly to the application narrative
- Research with a professor, major project with tangible output, leadership in an organization
- Also: draft Common App essay, research supplemental essay prompts, finalize college list
- "This summer has two jobs: do something meaningful AND get your application materials started."

### Step 4: Application Guidance for Selective Programs

For students targeting Tier 1/2 programs:

**Timeline**: Most applications open October-December, with deadlines January-March. Research early — some require teacher recommendations and transcripts.

**What they look for**:
- Demonstrated passion in the program's focus area (not just good grades)
- Specific, authentic interest — generic "I love science" won't cut it
- Evidence of initiative and self-direction
- Strong writing (essays matter as much as grades for these programs)

**How to strengthen applications**:
- Connect your application to specific things you've done (not just things you want to do)
- Be specific about why THIS program, not just any program
- Show what you'll bring to the cohort, not just what you'll get from it
- Get feedback on essays from a teacher or mentor who knows your work

**If not accepted**: "Not getting into RSI or SSP doesn't mean anything is wrong with your profile — these programs reject 95%+ of applicants. A strong self-directed summer can be just as powerful for your applications."

### Step 5: Self-Directed Project Guidance

Help the student brainstorm a substantive project connected to their spike. The project must have a **tangible output** — something they can point to and describe concretely.

**By track**:
- **STEM/Engineering**: Build an app, robot, or device. Conduct an independent research project. Create a technical blog documenting the process.
- **Business/Entrepreneurship**: Launch a small venture. Run a market research study. Organize a community initiative.
- **Humanities/Writing**: Write and publish articles, short stories, or a blog. Start a podcast. Create a documentary or oral history project.
- **Arts/Design**: Build a portfolio. Create a series (paintings, films, photographs). Organize a show or exhibition.
- **Social Impact**: Start a community organization or initiative. Run a fundraiser with measurable impact. Mentor younger students in your area.
- **Pre-Med/Science**: Shadow professionals (if accessible). Conduct an independent research project. Volunteer with a health organization.

**Guiding questions**:
- "What problem do you care about that connects to your interests?"
- "If you had a whole summer with no constraints, what would you build or create?"
- "What could you do this summer that would make you excited to talk about it in a college essay?"
