### Step 1: Current Activity Inventory

Ask the student to list every activity they are currently involved in. Collect one at a time to get full detail on each before moving to the next.

For each activity, ask:

1. "What's the activity?" (open-ended)
2. "What's your role? (pick one)"
   1. Member / participant
   2. Officer (secretary, treasurer, etc.)
   3. Leader (president, captain, editor-in-chief)
   4. Founder — I started it
   5. Other — tell me what
3. "About how many hours per week? (pick one)"
   1. 1-2 hours
   2. 3-5 hours
   3. 6-10 hours
   4. 10+ hours
4. "How long have you been doing it? (pick one)"
   1. Just started this year
   2. Since last year
   3. Since sophomore year
   4. Since freshman year or earlier
5. "Any achievements, awards, or impact you can point to?" (open-ended)

Also capture:

- Part-time jobs (these count — work experience demonstrates responsibility, time management, and often real-world skills)
- Personal projects (coding projects, art portfolios, YouTube channels, community initiatives)
- Family responsibilities (caregiving, translation for family members, managing a family business — these are legitimate and admissions officers recognize them)

**If the student has few or no activities:** Normalize it. "That's fine — many students are still figuring out what they want to be involved in. Let's use what you do have and find some great options to explore." Redirect energy toward discovery rather than guilt about gaps.

### Step 2: Spike Assessment

Cross-reference the student's activities against their academic track from `counseling_state.md` (if available). Apply the four-tier ranking from `references/academic-tracks.md`:

| Tier | Description | Examples |
|------|-------------|---------|
| **Tier 1: National/International** | Rare, national/international achievement | Intel Science Fair winner, USAMO qualifier, published research, national team |
| **Tier 2: State/Regional** | High achievement, significant leadership | Student body president, state competition winner, founded organization with measurable impact |
| **Tier 3: School/Institutional** | Meaningful involvement with some distinction | Club officer, varsity captain, Eagle Scout, regional competition finalist |
| **Tier 4: Community/Participation** | Solid participation | Club member, volunteer work, part-time job, school sports |

**Depth check for each activity:**

- Is there progression over time? (joined -> contributed -> led -> created impact)
- Is there measurable impact? (started something, won something, changed something, grew something)
- Does it connect to the student's academic track or spike area?
- Is the student genuinely engaged, or is this resume padding?

**Spike identification:**

Look for 1-2 activities where the student demonstrates the combination of: sustained commitment (2+ years), increasing responsibility, genuine passion (energy in their voice when they describe it), and measurable achievement or impact. This is the spike.

- **If a spike is clear:** Name it explicitly and explain why it stands out.
- **If multiple candidates exist:** Help the student identify which 1-2 feel most authentic and have the most growth potential.
- **If no spike is apparent:** Identify the strongest candidate activity and plan a path toward depth. Frame this as an opportunity, not a deficiency: "You have some solid involvement. The strategy now is picking one or two areas to go deeper in."

### Step 3: Gap Analysis

Compare the student's activity profile against what their track and target schools value. Reference the "Recommended Extracurriculars" section for the relevant track in `references/academic-tracks.md`.

**Gap categories to check:**

- **Depth gap**: Too many Tier 4 activities, not enough Tier 2-3 depth. "You're involved in a lot of things at a surface level. The strategy is to go deep in fewer things, not wide in many."
- **Leadership gap**: No leadership roles despite years of involvement. "You've been in this club for two years — have you thought about running for an officer position or starting a project within it?"
- **Track alignment gap**: Activities don't connect to the academic track. "Your course plan points toward engineering, but your activities are all in performing arts. That's not necessarily a problem — but we should figure out how to connect them, or whether your interests have shifted."
- **Community impact gap**: All activities are school-internal with no community reach. "Admissions officers want to see that you've had impact beyond your school walls. Have you thought about applying your [skill] in a community setting?"
- **Authenticity gap**: Activities that look like resume padding. Gently probe: "You listed six activities. Which ones would you keep doing even if colleges didn't care?" The answer reveals what is authentic.

**If gaps exist against track but the student has limited access** (rural area, low-income, no competitive programs nearby): Prioritize accessible alternatives — online competitions, virtual clubs, self-directed projects, local community initiatives. The equity check module applies here. Never assume the student has access to expensive programs or transportation to activities.

### Step 4: Strategy Recommendations

Based on the inventory, spike assessment, and gap analysis, provide targeted recommendations. Follow these principles:

**If spike exists — deepen it:**

- Identify the next level of achievement (school -> regional -> state -> national)
- Suggest competitions, certifications, or programs that raise the tier
- Plan for leadership if not already in a leadership role
- Connect the spike to a tangible project or output (research paper, app, nonprofit, event)

**If too many activities — prune for focus:**

- Recommend dropping Tier 4 activities that don't connect to the spike or bring genuine enjoyment
- The goal is not "more activities" but "fewer activities with greater depth and impact"
- Help the student decide what to keep: "If you could only keep three of these, which three would they be?"

**If no spike yet — build toward one:**

- Identify the 1-2 activities with the most potential (combination of interest, current depth, and growth opportunity)
- Plan specific actions to deepen involvement over the next 1-2 semesters
- Set a milestone: "By the end of this semester, aim to [specific achievable goal in the activity]"

**New activities — only if filling real gaps:**

- Suggest 1-2 new activities only if they fill a genuine gap in the profile AND connect to the student's interests
- Never recommend an activity purely because "it looks good on applications"
- For each suggestion, explain why it connects to the student's existing interests and track
- Always include free/accessible options

### Step 5: Progression Planning

Map grade-by-grade milestones for the student's spike and key activities. Adapt based on the student's current grade — only plan from the current semester forward.

**Progression framework:**

| Grade | Focus | Milestone |
|-------|-------|-----------|
| **Freshman** | Join and explore | Try 3-4 activities, discover what resonates, build relationships |
| **Sophomore** | Contribute and compete | Narrow to 2-3 core activities, take on committee roles, enter first competitions |
| **Junior** | Lead and create impact | Leadership positions, awards, measurable outcomes, peak competitive achievement |
| **Senior** | Culminate and mentor | Capstone projects, mentoring younger students, sustained meaningful involvement |

For the student's specific spike, create 2-3 concrete milestones for the next 1-2 semesters. These should be specific and achievable: "Apply for the section leader position by November" or "Submit a project to the regional science fair by March."
