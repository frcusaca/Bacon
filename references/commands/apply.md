# apply — Application Strategy Workflow

Use `references/admissions-knowledge.md` for application platforms, application types (ED/EA/REA/RD/Rolling), master application timeline, demonstrated interest strategy, and recommendation letter guidance. Use `references/timeline-engine.md` for application milestone tracking. Use `references/cross-cutting.md` for narrative threading module. Load `references/counselor-styles.md` and apply the student's active style to all question delivery, ordering, and phrasing.

### Prerequisite: College List Required

This command requires `schools` to have been run first. Check `counseling_state.md` for a populated College List section.

**If no college list exists**: "Before we plan your application strategy, we need a college list to work with. Want to run `schools` first to build one?"

**If the list exists but is very short (fewer than 4 schools)**: "Your current list has [N] schools. That's a start, but I'd recommend having 8-12 so you have a healthy mix of reach, match, and safety options. Want to expand the list with `schools` before we plan strategy, or work with what we have for now?"

### Step 1: Review College List

Pull the College List from state and confirm it with the student:

1. "Here's your current list: [list schools by category]. Is this still accurate? Any schools you want to add or remove?"
2. For each school, confirm the student's level of interest: "Are these still ranked in the right order for you? Which school would you be most excited to attend?"
3. Flag any list-balance issues:
   - All reaches, no realistic safeties → "I want to make sure you have options you feel good about no matter what happens."
   - Safeties the student doesn't like → "A safety school needs to be somewhere you'd genuinely enjoy. Would you actually go to [school] if it was your best option?"
   - No school type diversity → "Your list is all [type]. Have you considered a [different type] to compare?"

### Step 2: Application Platform Mapping

Map each school to its application platform (pull from `references/admissions-knowledge.md`):

1. **Common App**: Most widely used — 1,000+ schools. One base application, school-specific supplements. Recommend as default starting point.
2. **Coalition App**: ~170 schools focused on access. Check if any target schools prefer or require it.
3. **UC Application**: For all University of California campuses. Separate system, November deadline, 4 Personal Insight Questions.
4. **School-specific portals**: MIT, Georgetown, and others have their own applications. Flag these — they require separate work.
5. **ApplyTexas**: For Texas public universities.

For each school, note: "Here's which platform you'll use. Most of your schools are Common App, which means one base application covers them all — the main extra work is supplemental essays."

Remind about account creation: "If you haven't already, create your Common App account. The Common App typically opens August 1. You can start filling in activities, coursework, and demographic info before essays are ready."

### Step 3: ED/EA/RD Strategy Per School

Walk through application timing strategy for each school on the list. This is one of the most consequential decisions — take it seriously.

**Explain the options clearly** (from `references/admissions-knowledge.md`):
- **Early Decision (ED)**: Binding — if accepted, you must attend and withdraw all other applications. Deadlines typically November 1-15. Decision by mid-December.
- **Early Decision II (ED2)**: Second binding option at some schools. Deadlines January 1-15.
- **Early Action (EA)**: Non-binding early notification. Can apply EA to multiple schools.
- **Restrictive Early Action (REA)**: Non-binding but restricted — can't apply EA/ED elsewhere. Harvard, Stanford, Yale, Princeton.
- **Regular Decision (RD)**: Standard timeline. Maximum flexibility. January 1-15 deadlines, decisions March-April.
- **Rolling Admissions**: Apply as early as possible — spots fill continuously.

**For each school, recommend a strategy**:

1. **ED consideration** — only recommend if ALL of the following are true:
   - The school is absolutely the student's #1 choice
   - The student has thoroughly researched and ideally visited the school
   - Financial aid is not a deciding factor (cannot compare offers)
   - **Critical financial warning**: "ED is binding. If you're accepted, you'll get a financial aid offer at the same time — but you can't compare it to other schools' offers because you must withdraw those applications. If your family needs to compare aid packages, ED is usually not the right strategy." If the family's financial situation is unclear, err on the side of EA or RD.
   - If the school meets full demonstrated need (e.g., Ivy League, MIT), ED is less financially risky — note this.

2. **EA for schools that offer it**: "EA gives you early answers without commitment. You can apply EA to multiple schools. It's a strong strategy for reducing stress — you'll have acceptances in hand before the new year."

3. **REA consideration**: "Schools like Harvard, Stanford, Yale, and Princeton offer Restrictive Early Action. You can only apply REA to one school, and you can't combine it with ED or private EA elsewhere. You can still apply EA to public universities."

4. **RD as the default**: "Regular Decision gives you the most flexibility — you can compare financial aid offers, strengthen your application with senior fall grades, and submit your best work."

5. **Rolling admissions**: "For schools with rolling admissions, apply as early as possible. Spots and housing fill as decisions are made."

### Step 4: Timeline Mapping

Build a month-by-month action calendar for senior fall and winter:

**Use WebSearch to verify current deadlines** for the student's specific schools. Caveat: "These are based on recent cycles. Always verify exact dates on each school's website."

```
### Senior Year Application Calendar

**August**
- Common App opens (typically August 1)
- Finalize personal essay
- Begin supplemental essays for EA/ED schools
- Request transcripts from school counselor

**September**
- Complete and polish EA/ED supplemental essays
- Request recommendation letters (if not already asked)
- Finalize activity descriptions in Common App (150 characters each)
- Start preparing application for rolling admissions schools

**October**
- FAFSA and CSS Profile open (October 1) — submit as soon as possible
- Final review of EA/ED applications
- Submit rolling admissions applications

**October-November**
- Submit EA/ED/REA applications by deadlines (typically Nov 1-15)
- Begin RD supplemental essays
- UC application deadline (November)

**December**
- EA/ED decisions arrive (mid-December)
- If deferred from ED/EA: send update with new achievements (senior fall grades, new accomplishments)
- Continue RD supplemental essays
- If accepted ED: withdraw all other applications, celebrate

**January**
- Submit RD applications (deadlines typically Jan 1-15)
- ED2 deadlines (Jan 1-15)
- Submit any remaining FAFSA/CSS Profile forms

**February-March**
- ED2 decisions arrive
- Scholarship deadlines (ongoing — track individually)
- Wait for RD decisions (arrive late March through April)

**April**
- Compare financial aid packages across acceptances
- Appeal financial aid if needed
- Visit accepted schools if possible

**May 1**
- National Decision Day — commit to one school
```

Flag any deadlines that are approaching based on the current date: "Looking at today's date, your closest deadline is [X] on [date]. That's [N] weeks away. Let's make sure you're on track for that."

### Step 5: Demonstrated Interest Action Items

Pull the demonstrated interest strategy from the `schools` workflow (if it exists in state) and create a concrete to-do list:

1. For each school that tracks DI, list specific actions in priority order:
   - Sign up for mailing list and open emails
   - Attend next virtual info session (search for dates if possible)
   - Schedule campus visit (note: always sign in at admissions office)
   - Request/accept alumni interview if offered (always — even if "optional")
   - Attend college fair and connect with school's admissions rep
   - Follow up after events with brief thank-you email

2. For schools that don't track DI (Ivies, large publics): "Focus your energy elsewhere. Your application speaks for itself at these schools."

3. Prioritize: "Of your schools that track demonstrated interest, [School A] and [School B] should be your top priority because they're match schools where DI could make a real difference."

### Step 6: Recommendation Letter Strategy

Build a recommendation plan aligned with the student's narrative and spike:

1. **Who to ask** (from `references/admissions-knowledge.md`):
   - 2 core academic teachers (ideally from junior year, from different subjects)
   - Align at least one recommender with the student's intended major or spike area
   - Prioritize teachers who know the student as a person, not just a grade
   - "A teacher where you struggled then grew can write a stronger letter than one where you coasted to an A."

2. **Strategic alignment with narrative**: "Your spike is [area]. Who can speak to that passion from an academic perspective? And who can speak to a different strength — your character, your growth, your contribution to the classroom?"

3. **When to ask**:
   - Ideal: spring of junior year
   - Minimum: one month before the earliest deadline
   - For EA/ED (November 1): ask by early October at the absolute latest
   - "Ask in person, privately — not by email and not in a group."

4. **How to help recommenders write strong letters**: "Give each teacher a 'brag sheet' or brief resume. Include: your activities, accomplishments, goals, specific memorable moments in their class, why you chose them, your intended major, and your college list with deadlines."

5. **Counselor letter**: "Your school counselor will also write a letter. Make sure they know your story — schedule a meeting if they don't know you well."

### Step 7: Application Checklist

Build a per-school checklist of everything required:

For each school, track:
- [ ] Application platform and account created
- [ ] Application fee or fee waiver
- [ ] Personal essay (or UC PIQs)
- [ ] Supplemental essays (list specific prompts)
- [ ] Test scores (send official score reports — note which scores to submit per school)
- [ ] Transcript request submitted
- [ ] Recommendation letters requested (Teacher 1, Teacher 2, Counselor)
- [ ] Portfolio or audition materials (if applicable — art, music, theater programs)
- [ ] Interview (scheduled, completed, or not offered)
- [ ] Demonstrated interest actions completed
- [ ] FAFSA submitted listing this school
- [ ] CSS Profile submitted (if required by this school)

"This checklist keeps everything organized. We'll track progress in your state file so nothing falls through the cracks."
